Cataract and LASIK Surgery - Treatments & Therapies

Cataract surgery and LASIK surgery are two different procedures. Cataract surgery deals with replacing a cloudy lens in your eye with an artificial one, and LASIK surgery corrects nearsightedness, farsightedness, or astigmatism by changing the shape of your cornea.

Cataracts

Chances are, you know someone with a cataract or two. According to the National Eye Institute, part of the National Institutes of Health, by the time Americans reach 80 years old, 50 percent have cataracts or have had cataract surgery.

If the clear lens in your eye turns cloudy, you have a cataract. If cataracts don't affect your ability to drive or read, most eye doctors take a wait-and-see approach to treatment.

A cataract diagnosis doesn't mean you need surgery. If your cataracts are in the early stages, your ophthalmologist may recommend the following:

  • Getting a new prescription for your glasses
  • Buying unique sunglasses
  • Using a magnifying lens
  • Improving the lighting in your environment

These are options that can be exhausted before performing eye surgery. However, if your quality of life is being affected, your doctor may then recommend surgery.

Cataract Surgery

Your eye surgeon will operate on one eye at a time if you have cataracts in both eyes. When the first eye heals, the next surgery is scheduled. 

Thankfully, cataract surgery is pretty straightforward. Here are the basics:

  • A hospital stay isn't usually necessary, as the procedure is in the outpatient setting.
  • You're awake for the procedure.
  • Your eye doctor numbs your eye with a local anesthetic.
  • The surgery entails removal of the affected lens and replacement with an artificial lens.
  • The new artificial lens is permanent.  
  • If other eye conditions don't allow you to receive an artificial lens after yours is gone, your doctor will correct your vision later with new eyeglasses or contacts.
  • Healing takes about eight weeks.

Surgeries have a high success rate, but there's always a risk of bleeding or infection, as well as the chance of retinal detachment. 

Be sure to talk with your doctor if you're concerned about these risks.

New or Alternative Treatments: Medicated Eye Drops

Mice have been used to determine the success of medicated eye drops to treat cataracts, but according to the US National Institutes of Health’s J Fielding Hejtmancik, a researcher who specializes in eye disease genetics, "These are early studies and there's a lot of work to be done. But looking down the road, you can see that maybe within five to 10 years you might develop an agent that can slow the progression or even reverse early stage age-related cataracts." 

At this point, research is conducted on mice. Unfortunately, mice can't take an eye test and tell you if their vision has improved, so research continues.

Having cataracts is part of life for many of us. They only become a problem when they start to affect your ability to live a normal life. If your doctor recommends surgery, and you're not at the point where you want to take that step, you can wait. Putting off surgery will not create a bigger problem. There is no rush. Talk with your eye doctor about what's best for you. 

LASIK Surgery

LASIK surgery changes the shape of the cornea forever. The goal is to eliminate your glasses or contacts. It does not impact the lens of the eye, as cataract surgery does, only the cornea.

LASIK surgery is not for everyone. The American Academy of Ophthalmology and the FDA have come together to develop some guidelines to help determine if you are a good candidate for LASIK surgery. 

  • You must be 18 years old, or 21, depending on the procedure
  • Your eyes must be healthy with no recent changes in your prescription
  • There is a value range your order must fall between to be considered
  • You must be in good health; LASIK may not be a recommendation  for patients with certain autoimmune diseases, glaucoma, herpes of the eye, or cataracts
  • Consult with your eye doctor if any of these exceptions pertain to you.

Before the surgery, you'll have a complete eye exam to confirm the health of your eye and to make sure your cornea is thick enough to function after its shape is altered with the laser. During the surgery, a thin layer of your cornea is removed, which causes the shape to change based on the desired outcome; the only anesthetic used is numbing drops, but your eye doctor may give you something to relax. After the surgery, you may need a mild pain medication; an eye patch covers the eye until your follow-up eye doctor appointment 24-48 hours later.

Long-term Success of Your Surgery

Most people heal up and see a few days after surgery, but it could take three to six months before your vision stabilizes. In some cases, your eyes may have been undercorrected or overcorrected, and you may need another surgery to correct it.
